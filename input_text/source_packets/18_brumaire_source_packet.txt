18 Brumaire Multi-Source Process-Tracing Packet

Purpose

This is a curated multi-source packet for rerunning the process-tracing pipeline
on the French Revolution's terminal outcome: the 18 Brumaire coup and the
creation of the Consulate in November-December 1799. It is a deliberately
combined corpus, not one standalone historical document. It combines primary
legal and proclamation texts with secondary and critical accounts so the
pipeline can distinguish background
conditions, proximate decisions, military coercion, constitutional design, and
post-coup legitimation.

Research question

Why did the French Revolution culminate in Napoleon Bonaparte's 18 Brumaire coup
and the Consulate rather than a stable parliamentary republic, a revived
Jacobin-dominated republic, or a royalist restoration?

Temporal evidence sequence

1795: The Directory replaced the Thermidorian Convention. Source D says the new
regime was built around a five-member executive, the Council of Ancients, and
the Council of Five Hundred. Its constitutional design tried to avoid both
one-man tyranny and uncontrolled democratic radicalism.

1795: Source D describes the Directory's mission, in Antoine Thibaudeau's words,
as a "middle way" between monarchy and demagogy. This matters because the
Directory was not designed as a purely military regime; its collapse needs a
specific causal explanation.

1795-1797: Source D lists early stresses on the Directory: Babeuf's Conspiracy
of Equals, continuing insurrection in the Vendee, General Pichegru's royalist
defection, and the coup of 18 Fructidor Year V on 4 September 1797. These facts
support a structural-instability hypothesis, but they are temporally background
conditions unless tied to the 1799 decision window.

5 October 1795: Source D says Bonaparte gained regime confidence by suppressing
a royalist insurrection on 13 Vendemiaire Year IV. This is early agency evidence
for Bonaparte as a military solution available to civilian politicians.

2 March 1796: Source D records that the Directory appointed Bonaparte commander
of the Army of Italy. His later victories in Italy increased his popularity and
influence among elite political actors.

1796-1797: Source D says Bonaparte won against Austrian commanders at Montenotte,
Lodi, Arcole, and Rivoli, expanded French influence in Italy, and negotiated
with dynastic rulers. This supports the claim that he had exceptional military
prestige before Brumaire.

March 1798: Source D says the Directory appointed Bonaparte commander of the
Egypt expedition. This removed him from Paris before the final crisis but kept
him attached to military glory.

1-2 August 1798 and 1799: Source D states that the British destroyed most of the
French fleet at the Nile, the Cairo garrison faced revolt, plague spread during
the Syrian campaign, and the siege of Acre failed. These facts complicate a
simple hero narrative because Bonaparte's eastern campaign produced serious
military and logistical failures.

25 July 1799: Source D says Bonaparte defeated Ottoman forces at Aboukir. This
provided a success that could be reported in France despite the Egyptian
campaign's broader difficulties.

23 August 1799: Source D says Bonaparte left Egypt aboard La Muiron with trusted
men and generals, leaving Kleber in command. The timing is important: Bonaparte
returned to France during the Directory's political and military crisis.

8 October 1799: Source D says Bonaparte landed at Frejus and was cheered on the
way to Paris. The same source says France faced renewed war and the Second
Coalition. This provides both a popularity cue and a crisis cue.

May 1799: Source D says Abbe Sieyes was elected a director on 27 Floreal Year
VII, or 16 May 1799, and soon looked for constitutional change toward a stronger
executive.

1799 before Brumaire: Source D says Sieyes had support from financial-sector
figures Claude Perier and Jean-Frederic Perregaux. It also says he searched for
a military backer because he wanted a coup but needed force behind it.

1799 before Brumaire: Source D says Sieyes hesitated to bring Bonaparte into the
plot because he saw him as dangerous and ambitious. It adds that alternatives
were weak: Jourdan and Macdonald were too close to Jacobins, Moreau was
reluctant and suspected of royalist leanings, and Hoche and Joubert had died on
campaign. This is strong discriminating evidence for an elite-revisionist
mechanism: Bonaparte was recruited as a risky instrument, not initially as the
sole architect.

1799 before Brumaire: Source D says negotiations between Sieyes and Bonaparte
began in mutual distrust. Talleyrand and Roederer received concrete parts of the
plan as powerful safeguards and operators.

Weeks before 18 Brumaire: Source D says conspirators met at Bonaparte's apartment
on Rue de la Victoire to plan the coup. They set the date as 18 Brumaire Year
VIII, or 9 November 1799.

17 Brumaire Year VIII: Source D says Bonaparte invited General Lefebvre, commander
of the Paris garrison, and loyal officers to assemble at Rue de la Victoire at
7 a.m. the next day, armed and in dress uniform. This is proximate agency evidence
that military coordination was prepared before the official transfer to
Saint-Cloud.

18 Brumaire, 7 a.m.: Source D says the coup began with conspirators spreading a
rumor of a Jacobin plot. The rumor helped ratify a decree moving the assemblies
to Saint-Cloud.

18 Brumaire: Source D says Bonaparte received command of the army's 17th Division,
which was nominally to protect the assemblies. The same source states that this
gave him freedom to act.

18 Brumaire, around 10 a.m.: Source D says Bonaparte addressed the Council of
Ancients, defended his vision of the Republic, and said nothing in defense of
the Constitution. This directly supports a constitutional-break mechanism.

18 Brumaire: Source D says Ducos and Sieyes resigned as directors. Gohier and
Moulin suspected a trap and refused to resign. Bonaparte then used Moreau and
three hundred men to "protect" them at the Luxembourg, neutralizing opposition
inside the Directory.

18 Brumaire: Source D says Sieyes's military pawns were positioned across Paris:
officers watched the Ministry of War, Lannes and Berruyer guarded key sites,
Murat and Sebastiani watched the Palais Bourbon and Pont de la Concorde,
Macdonald went to Versailles, and Serurier moved the legislative guard to
Saint-Cloud. This is direct evidence of a planned coercive perimeter.

18 Brumaire: Source D says Barras waited to see which way politics would turn
until Talleyrand obtained his resignation. By the evening, the Directory had lost
its executive body.

18 Brumaire, evening: Source D says the conspirators still needed the assemblies
to vote for a new constitution. This is a key hoop test: if the plot were purely
military from the start, parliamentary ratification would matter less; if it was
a constitutional coup, assembly consent was initially central.

19 Brumaire: Source E says the second day was crucial because surprise had been
lost. Many Council members saw through the claimed threat that had justified the
move and suspected a trap.

19 Brumaire, early session: Source D says a significant Jacobin presence surprised
the plotters. The session began late, increasing tension. Debrel insisted that
deputies renew their vows to the Constitution of Year III, and they did so.

19 Brumaire: Source D says Bonaparte interrupted the Ancients, spoke incoherently,
and was poorly received. When challenged about the Constitution, he answered that
the deputies themselves had destroyed it through prior coups. The room erupted.

19 Brumaire: Source D says Bonaparte entered the Council of Five Hundred with
armed grenadiers, provoking cries against dictatorship, unlawful action, and
tyranny. The same source says Murat, Leclerc, and grenadiers prevented further
deterioration.

19 Brumaire: Source E says Bonaparte lost composure in the chambers, met growing
resistance, and almost fainted after the confrontation in the Council of Five
Hundred. This critical secondary account contradicts later heroic images of a
calm, surgical coup.

19 Brumaire: Source E says Lucien Bonaparte saved the day by calling on troops to
defend his brother. Source D similarly says Lucien left the assembly, addressed
soldiers theatrically, and helped justify military intervention.

19 Brumaire: Source D says Lucien declared to the soldiers that he would resist
his own brother if Napoleon violated French liberty. The speech reframed military
intervention as defense of liberty rather than coercion against deputies.

19 Brumaire: Source D says Murat entered the Orangerie and announced that the
deputies had been dissolved. Deputies panicked and some reportedly fled. This is
smoking-gun style evidence for the military-coercion mechanism because the
legislative body did not freely complete the process before force intervened.

19 Brumaire, evening: Source D says several hundred members of the Council of Five
Hundred were rounded up and a session finally began around 21:00. Chazal proposed
endorsing the two days' events, abolishing the Directory, and creating an
executive commission with Sieyes, Ducos, and Bonaparte.

10 November 1799: Source B says the Brumaire Decree declared that the Directory
was no more and named Sieyes, Roger-Ducos, and General Bonaparte as Consuls of
the French Republic. The decree also invested the commission with directorial
power, charged it with administrative order, internal tranquility, and peace, and
adjourned the legislative body until 20 February 1800.

10 November 1799: Source B says each council was to appoint a commission of
twenty-five members, and those commissions would handle urgent police,
legislation, and finance matters. This shows an effort to legalize the coup
through reduced legislative commissions rather than a full representative
assembly.

10 November 1799: Source B says the commissions were charged with constitutional
changes meant to consolidate sovereignty, the representative system, liberty,
equality, security, and property. This is official legitimating language. It
supports a republican-restoration frame but cannot by itself prove the actual
mechanism.

10 November 1799: Source A says Bonaparte's public justification claimed that
Parisian authorities were divided, that the Constitution was "half destroyed,"
and that all parties asked for his support. He presented himself as refusing to
be one party's man.

10 November 1799: Source A says Bonaparte claimed the Council of Elders summoned
him and gave him control of forces to secure legislative independence. This is
important as a self-exculpatory source: it is direct evidence of the official
narrative, not direct proof of the underlying cause.

10 November 1799: Source A says Bonaparte claimed assassins inside Saint-Cloud
threatened deputies, attacked him, and forced guards to clear the hall. This
claim is evidence about post-coup legitimation and news management; its
credibility should be tested against Sources D and E.

12 November 1799: Source B says the Consuls' proclamation framed the Constitution
of Year III as having perished because it could not protect rights or support
itself. It blamed ambitious factions for dividing the Republic and promised a
re-established republic founded on equality, liberty, and representation.

December 1799: Source C says the Constitution of Year VIII created citizenship
lists in which citizens chose trustworthy local names, then departmental lists,
then national lists. Public offices were drawn from these lists. This design
preserved a voting vocabulary while filtering actual selection through notables
and higher institutions.

December 1799: Source C says the Conservative Senate had eighty life members and
would choose legislators, tribunes, consuls, judges of cassation, and account
commissioners from the national list. The Senate's sittings were not public.

December 1799: Source C says Sieyes and Roger-Ducos, the retiring consuls, were
appointed to the Senate and would join with the second and third consuls to
appoint the majority of the Senate. This is direct institutional evidence of
elite self-reproduction inside the new constitution.

December 1799: Source C says the Tribunate could discuss proposed laws, but the
Legislative Body voted by secret ballot without discussion. The government could
withdraw or modify proposals during discussion.

December 1799: Source C names Bonaparte as First Consul, Cambaceres as Second
Consul, and Lebrun as Third Consul. It says the First Consul had special duties
and prerogatives.

December 1799: Source C says the First Consul promulgated laws and appointed or
dismissed ministers, high diplomatic agents, army and navy officers, local
administrators, and government commissioners before tribunals. The Second and
Third Consuls had consultative voices; after they recorded opinions, the First
Consul's decision sufficed. This is direct evidence that the final constitutional
outcome concentrated power in Bonaparte.

December 1799: Source C says the government proposed laws and made regulations to
secure execution. It controlled internal security, external defense, the armed
forces, and foreign relations.

December 1799: Source C says the public force was essentially obedient and no
armed body could deliberate. This institutionalizes military subordination to the
new state while the coup itself had relied on military intervention.

December 1799: Source C says constitutional authority could be suspended in cases
of armed rebellion or disturbances threatening state security. This provides a
legal emergency channel inside the new order.

December 1799 and after: Source E argues that the Bonapartist story turned the
coup into a myth of national rescue, but the Directory had more to offer than the
poor image propagated by successors. Crook says the coup nearly miscarried and
Bonaparte's authority was consolidated gradually rather than immediately.

1795-1799: Source E says the Directory had republican and pluralistic features:
a male taxpaying franchise of about five million, departmental electoral
colleges, annual legislative contests, some press freedom, and Year VII elections
that were endorsed rather than overturned. This evidence challenges an inevitable
collapse thesis.

1799: Source E says Bonaparte himself helped create the crisis because the
military reverses of 1799 followed from his adventurism in the Middle East. This
is direct counterevidence against a simple "Napoleon saved France from a crisis
he did not shape" narrative.

1799 before Brumaire: Source E says many recently elected deputies believed
constitutional revision was the alternative to Jacobin terror or royalist
restoration. This supports the claim that fear of extremes mattered to elites,
but does not by itself prove that fear made military dictatorship necessary.

1799 before Brumaire: Source E says Sieyes's original plan was to persuade the
two councils to yield power voluntarily to a revisionary commission. Bonaparte's
role was intended to be subordinate.

19 Brumaire and after: Source E says recourse to military force strengthened
Bonaparte's hand. He then shaped the new constitution in a more personalist
direction, creating what Crook calls a coup within the original coup.

December 1799: Source E says the Constitution of Year VIII dropped the Declaration
of Rights and made references to liberty and equality increasingly perfunctory.
This is interpretive evidence about authoritarian constitutional design, supported
by the constitutional provisions in Source C.

1799-1802: Source E says the new order was not immediately secure. Responses
ranged from republican relief to royalist expectation and widespread apathy. The
constitutional plebiscite showed indifference, and Crook says Lucien Bonaparte
effectively doubled disappointing vote returns.

1800-1801: Source E says broader popularity came later through victory at
Marengo, repression against lawlessness, and reconciliation with the Catholic
Church through the Concordat of 1801. This implies that the Consulate's durable
legitimacy was built after the coup rather than fully present before it.

Discriminating evidence map

Elite constitutional-revision mechanism. Strong evidence would show Sieyes and
revisionist civilians initiating the plan, recruiting a military figure because
constitutional change needed force, seeking assembly or commission approval, and
designing a stronger executive. The strongest packet evidence is Sieyes's May
1799 directorship, his search for a military backer, the lack of acceptable
alternatives to Bonaparte, the planned commissions, and the decree's
constitutional language.

Bonapartist personalist-military mechanism. Strong evidence would show Bonaparte
using armed control, personal prestige, Lucien's legislative role, Murat's force,
and the final Constitution to convert a broader revisionist plot into his own
rule. The strongest packet evidence is the assembly resistance on 19 Brumaire,
Murat's dispersal of deputies, Lucien's speech to soldiers, the evening roundup
of deputies, and the Constitution's concentration of appointment and decision
power in the First Consul.

Structural collapse/security-crisis mechanism. Strong evidence would show that
war, factionalism, prior coups, and fear of Jacobin or royalist threats made the
Directory unable to survive even without Bonaparte's individual actions. The
packet includes war, Second Coalition, prior disturbances, and official claims of
constitutional failure. But the critical sources also show the Directory had
ongoing elections, some pluralism, and possible liberal development.

Popular-demand/order mechanism. Strong evidence would show broad popular demand
for Bonaparte or mass legitimation before the institutional outcome. The packet
contains cheering at Frejus and official claims of republican salvation, but the
critical account stresses apathy, uncertain regional interpretations, and
post-hoc plebiscite manipulation. This mechanism should not win unless the
pipeline finds independent evidence of mass demand.

Royalist-restoration avoidance mechanism. Strong evidence would show elites chose
Bonaparte mainly to prevent restoration. The packet contains Pichegru's royalist
defection, fear of royalist restoration, and Bonaparte's earlier suppression of a
royalist insurrection. But the final institutional design empowered Bonaparte
rather than merely blocking royalists.

Source limitations and next evidence needs

This packet improves over a general encyclopedia-style input, but it is still a
curated excerpt packet. A publication-ready inquiry would add private
correspondence from Sieyes, Talleyrand, Roederer, Lucien Bonaparte, Moreau, and
Barras; minutes or voting records from the Councils; military orders and troop
movement records; police reports on the Jacobin-plot rumor; local press and
departmental responses; and raw plebiscite returns. These would test whether the
decisive mechanism was elite constitutional revision, Bonapartist military
coercion, structural regime collapse, fear of extremes, or popular demand for
order.
