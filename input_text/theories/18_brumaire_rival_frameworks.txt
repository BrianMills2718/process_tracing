Use these frameworks to sharpen rival hypotheses. Treat them as theory lenses,
not as source facts.

Framework 1: Elite coordination and constitutional revision.
When fragmented office-holding elites face a weak constitutional order, they may
coordinate around rule revision to protect property, offices, and order. This
framework predicts early civilian plotting, financial and administrative allies,
formal legal language, reduced representative bodies, and a stronger executive
designed before full military coercion becomes necessary.

Framework 2: Personalist military takeover and praetorian conversion.
When a prestigious commander controls troops at a moment of legislative crisis,
he can convert an elite constitutional plot into personal rule. This framework
predicts armed positioning, intimidation or dispersal of representative bodies,
family or factional brokers who translate force into legality, and a final
constitution that gives the commander disproportionate appointment and decision
powers.

Framework 3: Contingent institutional path closure.
Institutions can be weak but still have plausible development paths until
specific episodes close them. This framework predicts evidence that the Directory
retained some electoral, press, and republican capacity before Brumaire, and that
the decisive change occurred when the 19 Brumaire crisis transformed a planned
revision into a coercive constitutional break.
